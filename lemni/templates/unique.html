The Patented Lemnichain QA-LOS Cryptography

1. What does "NP" stand for? Is it the same as one-way functions?

NP (Nondeterministic Polynomial time): This is a fundamental class in computational complexity theory. Informally, it's the class of problems where a proposed solution can be verified quickly (in polynomial time), even if finding that solution might be very hard.

Example: The Boolean Satisfiability Problem (SAT). Given a complex logical formula, checking if a proposed assignment of TRUE/FALSE to its variables makes the whole formula true is easy. But finding such an assignment from scratch is believed to be very hard for classical computers.

Relationship to One-Way Functions (OWFs): They are not the same, but they are deeply connected.

Most practical one-way functions (like those used in RSA and ECC) are based on problems that are believed to be hard (e.g., factoring large numbers), but they reside in the class NP ∩ co-NP. This means both the problem ("find the inverse") and its complement ("this is not the inverse") have efficiently verifiable solutions.

The existence of strong, cryptographic one-way functions implies that P ≠ NP. If P equaled NP, it would mean that every problem with an efficiently checkable solution could also be solved efficiently, breaking one-way functions.

Quantum computers can break the one-way functions we currently use (factoring, discrete logs) by exploiting their algebraic structure. Shor's algorithm doesn't prove P = NP; it just shows that these specific "hard" NP problems are actually in BQP (meaning a quantum computer can solve them in polynomial time with high probability.).

2. What might cause one-way functions to be broken?

  • Quantum Computers (The immediate threat): Algorithms like Shor's algorithm can solve the integer factorization and discrete logarithm problems in polynomial time, completely breaking the one-way functions underlying RSA, ECC, and others.
  • Classical Mathematical Advances: Even without quantum computers, a breakthrough in mathematics could break a one-way function. For example, a new, incredibly fast factoring algorithm for classical computers would break RSA.
  • Flawed Implementation: The mathematical problem might be sound, but the way it's implemented in code could introduce vulnerabilities (e.g., side-channel attacks, poor randomness).

3. What do you call a system that doesn't rely on "novel hard" or "one-way" functions?

This is the key innovation of the QA-LOS paper. You wouldn't call it "NP proof" because NP is a massive class containing many easy problems. Instead, you describe it by its security foundation.

The correct term, which the paper itself uses, is that QA-LOS is based on Distributional Hardness or Sampling Hardness.

  • Old Paradigm (Broken by Quantum): Algebraic One-Wayness. Security relies on the computational difficulty of inverting a function (e.g., "Given c = mᵉ mod n, find m").
  • New Paradigm (QA-LOS): Distributional Indistinguishability. Security relies on the computational difficulty of distinguishing between two probability distributions. The "hard" problem is: "Can you tell if these output samples came from the complex LOS process or from a simple random number generator?"

4. How to refer to a system being invulnerable to these attacks?

You would say the system is immune to attacks based on algebraic inversion or that its security is not based on the one-way function paradigm.

Unlike traditional cryptography whose security is based on the one-wayness of algebraic functions vulnerable to Shor's algorithm, the QA-LOS framework derives its security from a Distributional Hardness Conjecture (DHC). Its security relies on the computational infeasibility of distinguishing its lemniscate-orbit sampling output from a uniform random distribution, a problem not known to be susceptible to quantum or classical algebraic attacks.

Conclusion

This pinpoints the most radical and defining aspect of the QA-LOS. Unlike other post-quantum algorithms (Dilithium, Kyber, Falcon, etc.):

QA-LOS is not just quantum-resistant; it is also algebraically agnostic.

Here’s the breakdown of what that means and the best terms to describe it:

The other NIST finalists (lattices, codes, multivariate) are still fundamentally based on the one-way function paradigm. Their security relies on the hardness of inverting an algebraic function:

  • Kyber (Lattices): Hardness of solving Learning With Errors (LWE) — finding a secret vector given a noisy linear system.
  • Dilithium (Lattices): Hardness of finding short vectors in a lattice.
  • Falcon (Lattices): Same as above.
  • Classic McEliece (Codes): Hardness of decoding a random linear code.

They are quantum-resistant because Shor's algorithm doesn't apply to these specific algebraic problems (for now). But they are still vulnerable to a future mathematical breakthrough (classical or quantum) that finds a way to invert their specific function.

QA-LOS is different. It completely abandons the inversion problem.

So, to complete your sentence:

"Unlike other quantum-resistant algorithms (dilithium, kyber, falcon) it is quantum resistant and immune to attacks on algebraic structure."

or more precisely:

"...it is quantum resistant and based on a fundamentally different hardness assumption: distributional indistinguishability instead of algebraic one-wayness."

Here are the key terms that capture this unique property:

  • Post-Quantum Secure via Sampling Hardness: This is the most precise description. It specifies how it achieves post-quantum security, which is different from everyone else.
  • Algebraically Agnostic / Structure-Agnostic: Its security does not depend on the hardness of a problem with a specific algebraic structure (like factoring, discrete logs, or finding short vectors). A break in one algebraic system does not imply a break in QA-LOS.
  • Resistant to Algebraic Cryptanalysis: This directly addresses your question about invulnerability. It is designed to be impervious to the entire class of attacks that exploit algebraic relationships and symmetries, which are the primary tools used against RSA, ECC, and even the lattice/code-based schemes.
